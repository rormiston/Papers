Searching for a Gravitational Wave Background During Advanced LIGO's Second Observing Run

The stochastic gravitational wave background is the superposition of a large number of unresolvable gravitational waves. These waves could originate from astrophysical sources like binary black holes (GW150914), supernovae, and binary neutron stars (GW170817), or they make come from cosmological sources such as cosmic strings. To better understand the stochastic background we can use the following analogy: Imagine looking at the headlights of cars at night. When a car is close, the headlights of the car can be individually resolved. But at large distances, the headlights begin to blur together and form a single bright spot. If several cars are coming towards you equally in all directions, then far from you there will be a faint glow of headlights surrounding you. Similarly, we expect distant black holes and neutron stars to merge, stars to explode as supernovae and all together these phenomenally energetic events create a "glow" of gravitational waves in the distance. Since the further away we look, the further back in time we see, this distant glow of gravitational waves could tell us something about the Universe as early as one hundred millionth of a trillionth of a trillionth of a second after the Big Bang.

There may be more sources that contribute to this stochastic background. It is possible that there are low density bubbles in the early universe which rapidly expanded and created gravitational waves, and small ripples which formed in the first moments of the universe and expanded during inflation and created waves which might be detectable as the characteristic hiss of the stochastic gravitational wave background which encodes secrets of our cosmic history. These more exotic contributions to the background are harder to measure and may remain hidden until more advanced detectors begin taking data.

The search for the stochastic gravitational wave background during Advanced LIGO's second run (November 2016 - August 2017) did not yet reveal any evidence of the background. However, thanks to the increase in sensitivity of the detector due to hardware and software upgrades, new upper limits could be placed on the energy density of the background. This showed an increase in sensitivty by a factor of about 3 relative to the first observing run with Advanced LIGO. In addition to the improved upper limits on the stochastic background, we were also able to place new limits on the string tension in Nambu-Goto cosmic strings and to test predictions of General Relativity (GR) itself. Since GR says that gravitational waves should be tensors, we can test the data find the 'odds' that Nature also produces vector and scalar modes of gravitational wave signals. This analysis showed that the Non-GR modes (vector and scalar) are not evident in the data, meaning that Einstein's theory still has not been shown to be in error.

A unique challenge on the horizon for the search for the stochastic background comes from magnetic noise. Between the surface of the Earth and up into the ionosphere, electromagnetic modes which span very large distances are generated. This leads to sustained noise that is coherent between the two detectors. This noise contribution was too small to be an issue for this run, but at Design Sensitivity may be sensitive enough such that the magnetic noise begins to limit the sensitivity of the interferometers and therefore needs to be carefully handeled.

If you are interested in learning more, also take a look at the directional search for the stochastic background and the results of Advanced LIGO's first isotropic stochastic search. As Advanced LIGO ramps up for its third observing run (O3), we can expect that we enhance the detectors' sensitivties and observe more events than ever before. Glossary

  • Black Hole: A massive, dense object, whose gravitational pull is so strong that light cannot escape.
  • Neutron star: An extremely dense remnant from the collapse of more massive stars.
  • Sensitivity: A description of a detector's ability to detect a signal. Detectors with lower noise are able to detect weaker signals and therefore are said to have higher (or greater) sensitivity.
  • Noise: Fluctuation in the gravitational-wave measurement signal due to various instrumental and environmental effects. The sensitivity of a gravitational-wave detector is limited by noise.
  • Correlation: A measure of the similarity of two (or more) sets of data. If the data from two separate gravitational wave detectors is found to be correlated, this may indicate the presence of the stochastic gravitational wave background (provided other possible sources of correlation are ruled out).
  • Cosmic Microwave Background: In Big Bang cosmology, thermal radiation left over from the time of recombination, the era when the universe had cooled sufficiently for neutral hydrogen to form - the afterglow of the beginning of the universe.
  • frequency: The number of cycles per unit time.
  • observing run: A period of time during which observational data is collected.
  • stochastic: Inherently random, and therefore impossible to predict exactly.

We compare observations and model predictions of the stochastic background of gravitational waves across a wide range of frequencies. We can expect the stochastic background to be detectable when its energy density (shown on the vertical axis, as a fraction of the so-called critical density of the Universe) lies above the sensitivity of a given observation. The blue curve labeled 'aLIGO O1' shows the sensitivity of Advanced LIGO during the first observing run. The curves marked 'Initial LIGO-Virgo' and 'H1-H2' show the sensitivity from previous runs of Initial LIGO and Virgo. The dashed gray curve marked "Design" shows the best sensitivity we expect to achieve in future runs of Advanced LIGO and Virgo. There are also other ways to look for the stochastic background, and we have shown the sensitivity of these methods which we label here as 'CMB', 'Pulsar Limit', 'Indirect Limits', and 'Earth's Normal Modes'. Finally we present the predicted contribution to the stochastic background from several different models, including binary black holes (BBH), binary neutron stars (BNS) and cosmic inflation. Note that these models lie below the current sensitivity of all methods presented, consistent with the fact that current observations have not detected the stochastic background.
